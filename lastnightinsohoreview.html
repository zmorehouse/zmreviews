Last Night in SoHo (2021)

Although I can‘t say I’m the biggest fan of Horror films, Psych-Thriller’s are my jam. I think there’s more substance in Thrillers - more intention behind their content than simply to scare, and ‘Last Night in SoHo’ does just this.

Edgar Wright’s take on a modern-day Hitchcock film marks some of his finest work since the Baby Driver. Featuring some incredible sequencing, jaw-dropping camera work,delightful colour grading and VFX and, in true EW fashion, a slue of eerie 60’s songs, ‘Last Night in SoHo’ is one of the most beautiful new-age thriller movies to date. Before I go any further, I dont think the trailer does this film justice - so if you plan on watching it, please go in blind.

The film follows Eloise, a fashion student that’s recently moved to the big city to persue her dream of becoming a designer.

However, after finding herself struggling to adjust to life in London, she finds herself being entranced by the dreamscape of SoHo - a late 60’s interpretation of the suburb she resides in and begins to live vicariously through Sandy, a wannabe celebrity from the time period.

I wont give you too much more, for scare of spoiling the film, however I did want to talk about some of the camera work and finer editing here. As I spoke about before, Eloise begins to live through the persona of Sandy. This is made obvious at the beginning of her nightly interactions, where she looks in the mirror and sees herself as the character.

However, this motif is constantly interpreted through the entire film. Hardly visible reflections of Sandy have been changed to Eloise - be it in the raindrops on the car window, or the cocktail shaker at the bar. These small details are incredibly effective in building this intimate relationship between the two and are a testimate to Edgar Wrights creative vision.

Furthering this intimacy is the pacing of the film. In true Hitchcock fashion - it‘s a bit of a slow burn. For the first half an hour of the film, you don’t realiseyou’re watching a thriller. This isn’t to say it’s bad, but the opposite. It does a phenomenal job of developing close relationships between it’s characters before inevitably descending into chaos.

The acting talent doesn’t dissapoint either, with Anya Taylor-Joy, Thomasin McKenzie and Matt Smith doing a phenomenal job of lending themselves to their characters, with the set design of 60’s London again reinforcing the acid-trip esque direction of Wright.

My biggest critique lies within some of the not-so-subtleties. The film is somewhat predictable, meaning a good chunk of viewers will guess the twist 20 minutes before it happens. The film is almost flawless, however the obvious foreshadowing and exposition (paired with some repetitive themes) detracts from the overall thrill of the film and it’s climax. Still, it’s a great watch - so give it a crack, and let me know what you think!
